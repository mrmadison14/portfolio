Case study

Letting an AI place hardware orders, safely

agent workflowsdocument parsingverificationhuman approval

The setup

Fleet hardware orders arrive as PDFs and form submissions: a customer wants forty tracking devices, a mix of harnesses, and everything shipped to the right depot under the right purchase order. For years the workflow was a person re-keying each one into vendor systems by hand. Slow, and worse than slow: hand-keyed orders fail in expensive ways. A shipment goes to a facility instead of the person who ordered it. A part number is one revision out of date. A kit gets ordered where the customer's contract requires its components.

I automated the intake with agent workflows. The interesting part is not the automation. It is the layer that keeps the automation from doing damage.

Extraction is the easy half

An agent reads the order document, pulls the line items, quantities, contacts, and PO details, and stages a complete order. Modern models are genuinely good at this. If the project had stopped there, it would have been a demo, and it would have shipped the same wrong orders as a rushed human, only faster.

The pre-flight layer

Before anything is written to a vendor system, the staged order runs a pre-flight checklist. Every rule in it exists because of a real failure mode, and any single failed check stops the batch:

  • Ship-to is the person who placed the order. Never a facility contact pulled from an address block. This one rule ended our worst error class.
  • Contacts match an existing record exactly. No fuzzy matching, and no reusing a contact across customers, ever.
  • Every part number is checked against the live catalog at order time. Catalogs drift: one harness SKU had been silently renamed with an -A revision suffix, and an order placed from the stale number would have bounced or shipped the wrong part.
  • Kit and bundle rules are per customer. Some contracts require ordering bundle SKUs intact; others require decomposing kits into components. The checklist knows which customer is which and refuses a mismatch.
  • Purchase-order naming follows one convention, with the customer's own PO number carried in a comment field, so finance can reconcile every order months later.
  • Orders mixing service plans are split into sub-orders, because the vendor system prices them wrong when combined.
The design stance

Automation earns trust by refusing to act. A staged order that fails one check produces a pass/fail report and stops. A human reads the report and decides. The system's job is to make that human review fast and its own mistakes impossible to miss.

What it does at scale

Order days used to mean one order at a time. Now a batch of twenty-plus orders gets parsed, staged, verified, and presented as a single review pass, and the person approving spends their attention on the judgment calls (is this quantity ratio odd? is this a pilot or a rollout?) instead of on retyping addresses. The judgment stays human on purpose: the checklist deliberately does not second-guess customer quantities, because that is the customer's call, not the tool's.

What I would tell you about it

When people ask whether AI can be trusted with real operations, they are usually asking about the model. Wrong layer. The model reads documents well. Trust comes from the verification harness around it: rules with a failure story behind them, checks against live systems instead of cached beliefs, and a stop that actually stops. Build the harness first and the AI gets to be useful; skip it and you have automated your mistakes.
