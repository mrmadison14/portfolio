Case study

A helpdesk migration the customers never saw

migrationshadow modemonitoringrunbooksknowledge base

The setup

The company had grown by acquisition, and support had grown with it: the original team ran one helpdesk platform, and an acquired brand arrived with its tickets, pipelines, and knowledge base living in a completely different one. Two queues, two tools, two versions of the truth. My job was to consolidate onto a single platform while both sides kept taking live customer traffic.

The constraint that shaped everything: customers were not allowed to notice. Each brand had its own support address, its own notification templates, and its own tone. A migration that leaked a stray "your ticket has been received" email from the new system before cutover would burn trust with exactly the people we were trying to keep.

The approach: shadow first

Rather than a big-bang cutover, I ran the new platform in shadow. Inbound traffic for the migrating brand was replicated into the target system, where the full future configuration (fields, pipelines, statuses, triggers, macros) processed it for real. Outbound was the dangerous half, so every customer-facing trigger was gated: shadow tickets flow through the whole machine, but nothing they generate may leave the building.

Shadow mode gave us weeks of evidence that the mapping was right before any customer touched the new system. Field maps and status maps were designed per brand up front, and the shadow traffic validated them against reality instead of against my assumptions.

The leak, and what it taught me

The first version of the gate keyed off a tag. A trigger stamped incoming shadow tickets with a marker, and every outbound trigger checked for that marker before firing. It worked in every test. Then a notification reached real customers anyway.

The cause was execution order. The platform ran triggers in sequence, and under specific conditions an outbound trigger evaluated before the trigger that applied the tag. The gate was checking for a marker that did not exist yet. Tests missed it because test tickets happened to take the path where the stamper ran first.

The rule I keep now

A safety gate must never depend on something another step has to do first. Gate on properties the ticket is born with (its brand, its origin), not on state applied during processing. Order-independence is the whole point of a gate.

I rebuilt the gating on brand identity, which exists the moment a ticket is created, and then went further: I wrote a leak monitor that watches outbound events from shadowed brands and alerts within minutes if anything escapes. The monitor caught the next configuration gap before any customer did, which is exactly the job I hired it for. Containment, apology, fix, hardened monitor. The incident became part of the design.

The knowledge base

The acquired brand's help articles lived behind an API that their plan tier did not expose, so export meant crawling our own content. I extracted 160 articles, remapped them into the target's structure (13 categories, 29 sections), and staged the load so the new help center could be reviewed as a whole before activation. Migrating content turned out to be the easy part; preserving its organization so agents could still find things was the real work.

Cutover discipline

The cutover itself ran from a written runbook: every step had a verification and a rollback, and hypercare monitoring ran for the days after with a dashboard the whole team could see. None of that is glamorous. All of it is the difference between a migration and a gamble.

What I would tell you about it

Monitors are not an afterthought to a migration; they are half of it. The shadow gate was designed to prevent the failure. The monitor existed because I assumed the design had a hole I had not found yet. It did. Assume yours does too.
